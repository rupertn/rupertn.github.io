Methodology


In this section, I will discuss how I arrived at the average value estimates shown in the table above. For many, I expect that understanding the technical intricacies of performing this analysis is not of great interest. However, to appease those that are interested, I will provide two methodologies, each at a different level of granularity.


Before moving on, you need understand how we assign a monetary value to Marriott Bonvoy points. When you redeem points for a stay at a Marriott property, the number of points required per night will depend primarily on the category of hotel. The higher the category, the greater the number of points required. To calculate the value we are recieving for our points redemption we need to compare the number of points required to the cost of purchasing the same room on the same night. For example, say I were to redeem points for a 1 night stay at a category 4 hotel (typically requiring 25,000 points per night) when the cash rate for a standard room was $200. In this instance, the value we recieve for our points works out to $200 / 25,000 points = 0.8 cents per point (cpp).


High-Level Explanation

Now, in late 2019, Marriott implemented a partial form of dynamic pricing of hotel redemptions. This came in the form of introducing off-peak and peak points pricing to hotel redemptions.


The first step was to collect hotel pricing data for all premium and luxury brand hotels under the Marriott Chain in North American cities. Once collected, the following process was applied to each hotel to estimate its average redemption value in cents per point:

  1. Find the cheapest available flexible rate (inlc. taxes and fees) on each date.
  2. Use statistical methods to detect and remove any signicant outliers in the list of cheapest flexible rates.
  3. Average the remaining flexible rates to find the average cheapest flexible rate.
  4. Divide the average cheapest flexible rate by the standard number of points required per night to estimate average redemption value.

If you rewind a couple of years, back to when global travel wasn't restricted due to COVID-19, and performed this analysis then, the methodology above would almost certaintly overestimate each hotels average redemption value. Normally, when travel demand is high, many popular hotels will sell out of redeemable rooms months in advance, meaning if we simply took the cheapest flexible rate it could represent an unredeemable room, which are often much priced higher.
